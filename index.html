STUDENT VIEWS ON

Student-Faculty Communication through Course and Teaching Evaluations (CTEs)

Matthew Kwan, Year 5 MBChB Student (Global Physician-leadership Scheme) , The Chinese University of Hong Kong
Dr. Carmen Wong, GPS Project Advisor, The Chinese University of Hong Kong

EMAIL
web maker
Student feedback through course and teaching evaluation (CTE) has been shown to be important for guiding course development. This feedback assists course administrators in evaluating the quality of teaching and learning. This project focuses on students' views on course evaluations, and the dialogue between students and the faculty.

METHOD

This is a qualitative study consisting of:

  • 20 Pre-clinical medical students from CUHK
  • 30-45 minute in-depth interviews
  • Interviews were transcribed and analysed for relevant themes

Purposive sampling
An equal proportion of male and female respondents were invited across all three preclinical years. Four class representatives were included to discuss in addition about their experience. As the majority of interviewees were students admitted via JUPAS (i.e. students holding local secondary school diploma), another four students admitted through non-JUPAS (i.e. students with qualifications of IB, GCE, AP, and postgraduates) were invited to give their views.

THEMES

Themes identified from the interviews included:

  1. Willingness to Participate 
  2. Modalities and Logistics
  3. Fitness for Purpose
  4. Faculty Student Communication

1. Willingess to Participate

Students were willing to participate in CTE for various reasons. Most students stated that it was important because it benefits students who will take the course in coming years. Also, evaluation was described as an official channel through which students can communicate with the faculty. Some participated in CTE for more personal reasons, for example expressing their thoughts on the curriculum for the year, or because they regarded the CTE as an opportunity to evaluate their learning.
Some students held a reserved attitude to evaluations. Some were uncertain whether their input was noticed given the large amount of student feedback the faculty handles in a year. Others have found current evaluations ineffective as they perceived it unrelated to recent changes in the curriculum.

2. Modality and Logistics

Students showed willingness to participate in CTE. They expressed that the time given for completing the forms was insufficient. They felt pressured by other factors, like staff members waiting by the door. Students explained that under the perception of limited time, they did not fill in the form with as much detail as they intended to.
Students have suggested collecting feedback online, as this allows submitting feedback at their convenience, without the limitation of time. The students who were willing to give their feedback in detail suggested that this allows them to express their full opinion. Students also expressed that they found typing online evaluations easier than filling in conventional evaluation forms by hand.

3. Fitness for Purpose

Students have found a discrepancy between questions on the CTE form and the teaching activities they receive. The source of discrepancy appeared to come from the teaching format, as the current medical curriculum involves new formats such as micromodules and flipped classrooms. The CTE form, on the other hand, is based on a "lecture and tutorial" format. Students reported this as a reason of their difficulties in filling out evaluation forms, particularly on aspects such as teaching format and teaching styles of individual teachers. Students also stated that questions such as language used, IT resources and library resources were not relevant to their learning.
 
Students also wished to write qualitative explanations after making their choices on Likert scales. As questions are based on Likert scales, students were unable to describe their opinion in detail. Students suggested including more free input in the evaluation forms, for example short answer questions, so that they can write evaluations in their own words. Students saw that such questions may minimize the difference between student opinion and CTE results.

4. Faculty Student Communication

A majority of interviewees stated the importance of student-faculty communication. They viewed CTE as a major channel of collecting student feedback, and saw the difficulties involved in addressing every piece of feedback obtained through this route. Alternative means of evaluation were discussed, including direct email contact with course coordinators, unofficial evaluations held by class representatives, and small group discussions with academic advisors.
Students from year 1 have mentioned academic advisors as an effective channel of communication with the faculty. Advisors have discussed with students their views on courses and alternative teaching activities, for example micromodules and flipped classrooms. Likewise, students preferred small group discussions among their academic advisory group. They found it easier to discuss their opinion in a small group setting, where the atmosphere was perceived as less judgmental.
Students of senior years, who were not under the new academic advisory system, mentioned unofficial feedback collection by class representatives as a favorable alternative to CTEs. The questions on the online form set up by class representatives were said to be in line with the medical curriculum. Students also found evaluations by class representatives beneficial to their anonymity. 
In general, students found reassurance when results from CTEs were addressed in public by course coordinators. Students who recalled such events were willing to write more on CTEs as they felt that course coordinators received the CTE results. Students also suggested releasing CTE results to students so that students can see their input being processed by the faculty.

SUGGESTIONS

Create Suitable Tools

Evaluation tools can be modified to accurately capture students' contributions.

1. Tailor evaluation questions to course objectives and special features/ modalities of teaching
2. Include subitems for questions on general attributes
3. Include more open-ended options

Facilitate Participation

It is also important to create an environment that facilitate participation.

1. Evaluations to be done at designated timeslots with adequate time provided
2. Students to be given more time to formulate their reflections
3. Consider implementing online CTEs
